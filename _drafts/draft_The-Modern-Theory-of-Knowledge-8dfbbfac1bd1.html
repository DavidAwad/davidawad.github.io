The Modern Theory of Knowledge


So what does it mean for someone to know something? 

Can you define it without using an example? 

How?

Since you’ve probably looked it up by now, knowledge is defined as, “facts, information, and skills acquired by a person through experience or education; the theoretical or practical understanding of a subject.”

So what does it mean for someone to properly be said to know something? 

Maybe you’d simply say that to know something is to be aware of something that is true. You could argue, given that the word stated that knowledge consists of facts. Well that’s a fine definition… sort of. 

Here’s the problem. When you assert that knowledge is simply what is factual. That changes with time. In fact, it was not too long ago that “fact” was that the earth was the center of the universe. Facts change with time as new information comes to light. So we must adjust our definition. 

What could we do to make our definition modular in order to adjust for the changes in facts. How can we do this? 

Well, this is where there’s some variation. 

The field remained this way until around the 1950's when Edmund Gettier wrote a fantastic article (one of the shortest in the history of philosophy mind you) on the nature of disjunctive knowledge. 

“Gravity is a Force, and Carbon is an element.” These are two separate statements, that are both knowledge individually. So can we assert that the two statements put together is also a single statement of knowledge?
